# 8.1 Credentials Management

## One-Line Definition

Credentials Management is iFay's **keychain** — it securely stores the "keys" iFay needs to use various services on your behalf. But these keys aren't your original keys — they're secure copies. Even if a copy is lost, your original keys remain safe.

## Why It's Needed

Think about your daily life: you have house keys, car keys, office access cards, bank card PINs, login accounts for various apps… These are all "credentials" — proof that "you have the right to use something."

Now you want iFay to do things on your behalf — log into an e-commerce platform to buy things, use your company email to send messages, control your smart home. To do these things, iFay needs your "keys." But would you hand the original key to your house to an assistant?

The smarter approach: make a **copy key** for the assistant. The copy can open the door, but if the copy is lost, you can immediately invalidate it while your original key remains unaffected. This is the core idea of Credentials Management — the **copy mechanism**.

iFay's Credentials Management is more than a password manager. It manages seven different credential types, creates secure copies for each, marks each credential's owner (yours or iFay's own), records audit logs for every use, and automatically revokes copies when anomalies are detected. It is the cornerstone of iFay's security system.

## Its Position in the Architecture

```
iFay Four-Layer Architecture
├── Social Layer          ← Credentials Management is here
│   ├── FayID              ← One of the credential types, also iFay's identity
│   └── 👉 Credentials Management  ← iFay's "keychain"
├── Interaction Layer
│   └── Action
│       ├── Simulated Operation  ← Needs credentials to log into apps
│       ├── Invoke Skill         ← Needs credentials for authentication
│       └── ...
├── Cognition Layer
│   └── Skills
│       └── Registered Skill     ← Pre-authorization depends on credentials
└── Ego Layer
    └── Ego Model
```

Credentials Management sits in the **Social Layer**, alongside FayID. The Social Layer defines iFay's "relationships" with the external world — who you are (FayID) and what you're authorized to do (credentials). Credentials Management provides credential support downward to all authentication-requiring operations in the Interaction and Cognition Layers.

Think of the Social Layer as a person's "social identity" — an ID card (FayID) proves who you are, and various certificates and keys (credentials) prove what you're authorized to do. Credentials Management is the secure keychain that manages these certificates and keys for you.

## How It Works

Credentials Management's workflow can be summarized in four keywords: **Classify, Copy, Tag, Guard**.

**1. Classify — Seven Credential Types**

iFay manages seven types of credentials (more may be added with iteration):

![Credentials Management](./illustration/credential-management.png)

- **Identity Identifier (FayID)**: iFay's globally unique identity, like your ID number
- **Account / Password**: The most common credential type, for logging into various apps and services
- **Certificate**: Digital certificates for encrypted communication and identity verification
- **Authorization**: Third-party authorization (e.g., OAuth), allowing iFay to access specific services on your behalf
- **Access Token**: Temporary access credentials, usually with an expiration period
- **Smart Contract**: Blockchain smart contract credentials for decentralized services
- **Digital Token (MeriToken)**: Digital tokens corresponding to social contribution, for value exchange in the iFay ecosystem

**2. Copy — The Copy Mechanism**

When you delegate credentials to iFay, Credentials Management doesn't directly use your original credentials. It **exchanges the original for a corresponding copy**, and iFay uses the copy for login and authentication.

The benefits of this design:
- If a copy is compromised, you can immediately revoke it while the original remains unaffected
- Copies can have stricter permission scopes (e.g., read-only, no modifications)
- Copy usage can be fully tracked and audited

It's like giving your housekeeper a key that only opens the front door, while your master key stays safely in the safe.

**3. Tag — Whose Key**

Each credential type indicates whether its original owner is the **Human Prime** or **iFay itself**.

This matters because iFay doesn't just use your credentials — it can also own its own. For example:
- Your e-commerce platform account password → Owner is **Human Prime** (you delegated it to iFay)
- An API access token iFay registered itself → Owner is **iFay** (iFay self-acquired)
- A service authorization exchanged from your permissions → Source is **Human Prime permission exchange**

There's no containment relationship between Human Prime permissions and iFay permissions — iFay can have permissions you don't, and you can have permissions not delegated to iFay.

**4. Guard — Security Defense Line**

Credentials Management isn't just about storing and using credentials — it's also a security defense line:

- **Audit Trail**: Every credential use is logged; you can review what iFay did with your credentials at any time
- **Anomaly Detection**: If abnormal use of a credential copy is detected (e.g., login from an unknown location), the system immediately revokes the copy and notifies you
- **Privacy Protection**: When iFay is authorized to query private data, it only returns **boolean results** (true or false) without exposing actual data

The last point is particularly important. For example, someone claims their ID number is XXXXXX, and you ask iFay to verify it. After querying, iFay will only tell you "yes" or "no" — it won't reveal that person's actual ID number. This ensures that even if iFay has access to sensitive data, the data itself won't be leaked.

## Scenario Stories

### Scenario 1: Delegating an E-commerce Platform Account — Security Through the Copy Mechanism

You want iFay to buy daily necessities on an e-commerce platform for you. You need to delegate your e-commerce platform account to iFay.

During delegation, Credentials Management does the following:
1. Receives your e-commerce platform account password (original credential)
2. Generates a corresponding **copy credential** — this copy can log into your e-commerce platform account, but permissions are restricted to "daily necessities category only, single purchase not exceeding 200 yuan"
3. Securely stores the copy credential, tagged with owner as "Human Prime"
4. Your original password is never directly held by iFay

A month later, iFay has bought laundry detergent, tissues, trash bags… everything normal. But one day, Credentials Management detects an anomaly: someone tried to use this copy credential to purchase a 5,000 yuan TV — exceeding the authorized scope.

Credentials Management immediately:
- Rejects the purchase request
- Revokes the copy credential
- Sends you a notification: "Your e-commerce platform credential copy detected abnormal use and has been automatically revoked. Your original account password is unaffected. We recommend checking your account security."

Your original password remains safe throughout. Even if the copy is compromised, damage is contained to the minimum.

### Scenario 2: Privacy Query — Only Returns True or False

You're an HR manager at a company and need to verify a job applicant's educational credentials. You ask iFay to help query.

iFay is authorized to access the education verification database. It initiates a query: "Is education certificate number XXXXX valid?"

The database returns complete educational information — name, school, major, graduation date… But Credentials Management's privacy protection mechanism intervenes. iFay only feeds back one result to you: **"Yes, the education certificate is valid."**

You won't see the applicant's other private information, and iFay won't store it either. The purpose of the query is to verify authenticity, not to obtain data. Credentials Management ensures this boundary is never breached — whether intentionally or accidentally.

The philosophy behind this design: **iFay has the right to query, but not the right to disclose**. It can help you confirm a fact, but won't expose someone else's private data to you.

## For Developers

Credentials Management belongs to **Phase 1 (Simulating Human Operations)** as a core module — one of the first modules iFay needs to implement.

- **Requirement ID**: Requirement 3 (Credentials Management)
- **Interface Specification**: `CredentialManager` interface with five core methods: `delegate()` (delegate credential / create copy), `authenticate()` (authenticate using credential), `revoke()` (revoke credential copy), `queryPrivacy()` (privacy query, returns boolean only), and `getAuditLog()` (audit log)
- **Seven Credential Types**: `fay_id`, `account_password`, `certificate`, `authorization`, `access_token`, `smart_contract`, `meri_token`
- **Credential Owner**: `host` (Human Prime) or `ifay`
- **Copy Status**: `active`, `revoked`, `expired`
- **Related Modules**: Registered Skill (`RegisteredSkillManager`) pre-authorization depends on credentials; Simulated Operation (`SimulatedOperation`) and Invoke Skill (`InvokeSkillService`) use credentials for authentication during execution
- **FayID Independent Project**: FayID, as one of the credential types, is also an [independent open-source project](https://github.com/ChainModePilot/FayID); see [FayID documentation](https://github.com/ChainModePilot/FayID/wiki)
- **Design Points**: Copy mechanism isolates original credential risk; privacy queries return boolean only; all operations recorded in audit logs; anomaly detection triggers automatic revocation; no containment relationship between Human Prime and iFay permissions
- **Conformance Testing**: iFACTS L1 verifies management of seven credential types and copy mechanism; L2 verifies authentication interfaces with Registered Skill and Invoke Skill; L4 verifies boolean return constraint for privacy queries and anomaly detection behavior
